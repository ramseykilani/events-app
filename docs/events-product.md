# Events — Product Overview

---

## The Problem

You want to go to a concert on Friday. You'd love company. So you open your phone and message 10 or 15 people individually: "Hey, I'm going to this thing — want to come?" It's tedious. It's friction. And it happens every single time.

Some people solve this with a group chat called "Events" where they broadcast what they're attending. But that has an obvious limitation: the group chat only contains *your* friends. When your friend posts their event in your chat, they're broadcasting to your network, not theirs. They don't have a group chat like this, and even if they made one, they'd be managing yet another chat thread in yet another app that's fighting for their attention.

Events solves this. One tool. No friction.

---

## What It Is

Events is a calendar-based app for sharing events with the people in your life. That's it.

You open the app. You see a calendar. On that calendar are events that your people have shared. Each event includes a link to the public listing where you can buy a ticket or sign up. If you want to share it with your own people, you tap share. If you want to coordinate with someone, you already have their number — text them.

There is no feed. There is nothing to scroll. There are no likes, no comments, no RSVPs, no read receipts, no engagement metrics.

---

## Your People

You curate an in-app contact list of up to 50 people — your people. These are the people you share events with and who share events with you. This is not your full phone contact list. It's the people you actually want to exchange events with.

Circles are optional shortcuts. You can group your people into circles — a rave group, a theater crew, a dinner circle — so that when you share an event, you can quickly select a whole group instead of tapping 15 names individually. A person can be in multiple circles or no circles at all. Circles are a convenience, not a requirement.

The 50-person cap is intentional. It eliminates influencer dynamics. It keeps the list manageable. It means every person on your list is someone you'd plausibly want to share an event with. This isn't broadcasting to an audience — it's sharing with your people.

If you haven't shared an event with someone in six months, they're quietly removed from your list. No notification, no guilt. They just fade out, and your list gets a little cleaner. You can always add them back.

---

## Core Screens & Flows

### The Main Screen

The main screen is a calendar. You see events that your people have shared, displayed on the days they're happening. You can see who shared each event. Each event links out to the public listing. A floating action button lets you share a new event. There is nothing else to do on this screen.

### Sharing an Event

You tap the button, paste a link, confirm the title and date, and pick who to share it with — individual people, circles, or any combination. If you see an event on your calendar that someone else shared and you want to pass it along to your own people, you tap it and hit share. Same flow, no re-entry.

### Notifications

When someone shares an event with you, you receive a push notification showing the event title and date. Tapping it takes you directly to the event detail screen. This is the only notification the app sends — it is always triggered by another person, never by the app itself.

## Onboarding & Growth

### Signing Up

You sign up with your phone number. You select up to 50 contacts from your phone — these become your people list. You can optionally organize them into circles. If someone is already on the app, the connection is live immediately. If they're not, that's fine — they're on your list, and as soon as they sign up, everything just works without you having to do anything.

### The One-Friend Bootstrap

You're likely downloading Events because at least one friend told you about it. That friend is already on the app. So from day one, you have at least one person who can share events with you, and who you can share with. The app is already useful. It has already eliminated the need to text that one person every time you find something interesting.

From there, the network grows naturally. Your friend tells their friends. Those friends bring their people. The node network expands outward — or it doesn't, and the app serves you and 15 friends and reduces your collective friction, which is a perfectly good outcome.

**Events does not need to grow to work.** It provides value to a single user with a single connection. There is no critical mass required, no network effect threshold to hit before it becomes useful. It works on day one.

## Hiding People

Sharing is asymmetric. You might want to share events *to* someone while not wanting to see what *they* share. Without a hide feature, the only option is to remove them from My People entirely — but that also removes your ability to share events *to* them.

Hiding someone means their events don't appear on your calendar and you don't receive notifications from them. It carries no social implication beyond that. The hidden person is completely unaware and entirely unaffected — it's purely a calendar filter.

You can only hide someone from within an event they shared with you. This keeps the action contextual: you're responding to something that actually happened. The Hidden section in My People is for undoing a hide only — you can't hide someone from there.
